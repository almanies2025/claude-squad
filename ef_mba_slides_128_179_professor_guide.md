# EF MBA Sessions 2–4: Early Stage Financing

## Slide-by-Slide Professor Guide — Slides 128 to 179

## SLIDE 128: Liquidation Preference

**Content Summary:**
Two components — preference and participation. Preference = actual claims before other classes. States a multiple of the original investment returned before common stock receives any consideration.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Establishes the "waterfall" concept — who gets paid in what order
- Preference multiple is typically 1x, 1.5x, or 2x of original investment
- This is foundational to understanding VC deal economics

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"If a VC invests $2M for 20% of a company at a $10M pre-money valuation, and the company sells for $3M — with a 1x liquidation preference, who gets what? Does the VC get their full $2M back? Does the founder get anything? Draw the waterfall."_

---

## SLIDE 129: Types of Liquidation Preference

**Content Summary:**
Non-participating (simple) liquidation preference — entitles holder to a pre-determined portion equivalent to the amount originally invested. Takes either the preference payout OR converts to common — not both.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- The "either/or" nature of non-participating is critical
- Participating = double-dip, non-participating = single dip
- Most common structure in practice

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"As the entrepreneur: would you prefer non-participating or participating preference? As the VC: which do you prefer? What is the fundamental tension here — what is each side actually negotiating for?"_

## SLIDE 130: Liquidation Preference — Exercise

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Hands-on application of preference mechanics

At a 2M exit? At what exit value does the preferred shareholder prefer to convert to common instead of taking the preference?"_

## SLIDE 131: Example of Participation

**Content Summary:**
VC/PE invests $10M for preference shares convertible into 50% of the venture's stock. 1x preference, non-participating. VC gets 1x of original investment or $10M but does not participate in upside.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Sets up the comparison between participating and non-participating
- 50% ownership on an as-converted basis is very high — this is a founder-unfriendly deal
- The investor chose not to participate in this example

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"If the exit value is $30M, with 1x non-participating preference: does the VC take the $10M preference or convert to common? What do they receive under each option? Which would they choose and why?"_

## SLIDE 132: 1x Preference, Non-Participating

**Content Summary:**
Exit value diagram showing Series A (preferred) vs. Series A (Common). Below the crossover point, preferred wins. Above crossover, common wins. Illustrates the break-even point.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Visual representation of the trade-off
- The "crossover point" is where conversion becomes better than taking preference
- Non-participating preferred is economically similar to common above the break-even

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Point to the crossover on the diagram. At what exit value does it not matter whether the VC takes the preference or converts to common? What does this mean for the entrepreneur's incentive at different exit scenarios?"_

---

## SLIDE 133: 1x Preference, Participating

**Content Summary:**
Exit value diagram showing Series A (preferred) with double-dip: takes preference AND shares remaining pro-rata. Line is higher than non-participating at all exit values.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Participating preferred is always better for the investor than non-participating
- The double-dip is visible in the diagram — the preferred line starts higher AND has a steeper slope
- This is the most investor-friendly structure

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"At a $20M exit: calculate exactly what the participating preferred VC receives versus the non-participating VC. How much extra did the participating provision add in dollar terms? At what exit size does the participating VC achieve 2x their money?"_

---

## SLIDE 134: 1x Preference, Participating Capped at 2x

**Content Summary:**
Exit value diagram showing participation that caps out at 2x total return. Diagram shows the cap point and how it limits investor upside while still providing downside protection.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- The cap is the entrepreneur's negotiating win
- Without cap: participating preferred can achieve very high multiples at high exits
- With cap: entrepreneur retains more upside above the cap point
- This is a common compromise structure

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"If the VC caps out at 2x, at what exit value does the cap become binding? What happens above that exit? Is this a good deal for the entrepreneur or the investor? Defend your answer with specific numbers."_

## SLIDE 135: Liquidation Preference with Different Performance

**Content Summary:**
Level and structure of liquidation preference has huge impact on VC returns, particularly at moderate performance. Example: VC invests $2M for 20% at $10M valuation.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Liquidation preference matters most at moderate exits — not at very low or very high exits
- Very low exit: everyone loses regardless of structure
- Very high exit: common stock performs well regardless
- Moderate exit: structure determines who wins

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"At a $5M exit: what does the VC with 1x non-participating receive? What about 1x participating? What does the founder receive under each structure? Now at a $50M exit — do the structures still matter? Why or why not?"_

---

## SLIDE 136: Liquidation Preference with Multiple Series

**Content Summary:**
Gets complicated with Series B, C. Two approaches: stacked preferences (Series B on top of Series A) vs. pari passu (equal ranking). Negotiation point.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- In multi-round companies, later investors negotiate for better position
- Stacking = junior preferred gets paid after senior preferred
- Pari passu = all series share equally in liquidation
- Seniority matters enormously in downside scenarios

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Series A has 1x non-participating. Series B invests at a lower valuation and demands 1x non-participating stacked senior to Series A. At a $10M exit: Series A invested $5M, Series B invested $3M. Who gets paid first? Who gets what? What would Series A have demanded at the time of their investment to protect against this?"_

---

## SLIDE 137: Focus on Value Not Valuation

**Content Summary:**
High valuation pursuit can be counter-productive. Liquidation preference level signals VC's view of current valuation vs. future potential. Preference = insurance against poor performance.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Key insight: preference level is a SIGNAL about how the VC views the deal
- Demanding high preference = "I think you're overvalued"
- Entrepreneurs who push for high nominal valuation with high preference have made a bad trade
- The real cost of high valuation is often paid through preference terms

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Professor insight: 'A VC demanding 2x liquidation preference is basically telling the entrepreneur: I don't believe your valuation.'辩论这个问题：如果创始人接受高估值但附带严格的清算优先权，这对创始人实际上意味着什么？他们是否真的"赢得"了高估值谈判？"_

## SLIDE 138: Anti-Dilution

**Content Summary:**
Used to protect investors when firm issues equity at lower valuation (down-round). Two key scenarios. Can also be used for controls. Recent developments.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Down-round = new shares issued at lower price than previous round
- Anti-dilution compensates earlier investors for the price drop
- Without anti-dilution: earlier investors suffer economic dilution
- With anti-dilution: earlier investors receive additional shares to maintain their economic position

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"You raised Series A at $3M valuation. A year later, desperate for cash, you raise Series B at $2M valuation. Without anti-dilution: what happened to your Series A investor who paid $3M? With anti-dilution: what should happen? Now: who benefits from anti-dilution — the investor or the entrepreneur?"_

---

## SLIDE 139: Anti-Dilution (continued)

**Content Summary:**
Besides economic impact, anti-dilution could be used for controls. In some cases, firms will forego additional investments at lower valuation. Recent development noted.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Anti-dilution has a control dimension — it can deter founders from accepting low valuations
- Firms may turn away financing rather than trigger anti-dilution
- This is an underappreciated strategic tool

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"If you're a founder and know your Series A investor has full ratchet anti-dilution — and you're struggling to raise a Series B — what are your options? What are the consequences of each? Does anti-dilution change your behavior even before a down-round happens?"_

---

## SLIDE 140: Anti-Dilution — Full Ratchet Adjustment

**Content Summary:**
In full ratchet, the conversion price is adjusted in line with the new investor's conversion price. Example: Series A investor bought at $3, market fallen to $2 (Series B).

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Full ratchet = most aggressive anti-dilution for investors
- Series A conversion price is adjusted ALL THE WAY DOWN to Series B price
- $3 becomes $2 — full adjustment with no floor
- Founders almost always lose this negotiation if agreed to

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Series A at $3/share. Series B at $1/share. With full ratchet: what is Series A's new conversion price? How many shares does Series A investor receive for their original $9M investment? What percentage of the company do they now own? Is this fair? Why would a founder ever agree to this?"_

---

## SLIDE 141: Anti-Dilution — Weighted Average (Narrow)

**Content Summary:**
Narrow weighted average: only the new shares issued at reduced price are considered. Formula: NCP = (OCP × OS + NSM) / (OS + NS)

**Key Teaching Points:**

- NCP = New Conversion Price
- OCP = Original Conversion Price
- OS = Original Shares
- NS = New Shares
- NSM = New Shares × new price (dollar amount)
- Less punitive than full ratchet — tapers the adjustment

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Walk through the formula step by step with numbers: Series A: $3/share, 3M shares ($9M). Series B: $2/share, $2M investment = 1M new shares. NSM = $2M (dollar amount). Calculate: NCP = ($3 × 3M + $2M) / (3M + 1M) = $11M/4M = $2.75. How many extra shares does Series A receive? What is their new ownership %?"_

---

## SLIDE 142: Anti-Dilution — Example

**Content Summary:**
$9M invested at $3/share = 3M shares. With weighted average: each original share priced at $2.66. Investor gets ~3.38M shares (0.38M free shares). Neutralizes negative wealth impact.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- The 0.38M free shares compensate for the dilution
- Investor's dollar value stays approximately the same ($9M / $2.66 = 3.38M shares × $2.66 = $9M equivalent)
- Anti-dilution is designed to be economically neutral — it doesn't give the investor a windfall

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Note: the slide says $2.66, but our calculation gave $2.75. Verify: does NSM = $2M (dollar amount) or does it = NS × price? If NS × price = 1M × $2 = $2M, we got $2.75. Where does $2.66 come from? (This is a good exercise in reading formulas precisely — NSM is defined differently in different textbooks.)"_

---

## SLIDE 143: Anti-Dilution — Weighted Average (Broad)

**Content Summary:**
Broad weighted average: uses fully diluted share count (OQ / NQ). Formula: NCP = OCP × (OQ / NQ)

**Key Teaching Points:**

- OQ = Original Qualified shares (fully diluted, including all options, warrants, reserved)
- NQ = New Qualified shares (fully diluted after new round)
- Broad is more founder-friendly than narrow because it uses a larger denominator
- Fully diluted basis means more shares in the denominator → higher NCP → less dilution

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Same numbers: Series A 3M shares. But also 1M options/reserved shares = 4M fully diluted. Series B: 1M new shares. Calculate NCP with broad formula: NCP = $3 × (4M / 5M) = $2.40. Compare to narrow: $2.75. Which is better for the founder? Which is better for the investor? Why does the formula difference matter?"_

## SLIDE 148: Pay-To-Play (PTP)

**Content Summary:**
Aimed at penalizing investors who do not participate in future financing. If all prior investors agree, some preferential rights are cancelled to encourage additional investments. Useful when firm is struggling.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- PTP solves the "zombie investor" problem — investors who sit on losses and refuse to participate
- Non-participating investors lose preferential rights if they don't co-invest in future rounds
- Forces investors to "put up or shut up"

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"As an investor: would you accept a PTP clause? What are you implicitly committing to at the time of initial investment? As a founder: why would you want this clause in the term sheet? Who is the real beneficiary of PTP — the founder or the new investor?"_

## SLIDE 149: Key Control Terms in Term Sheets

**Content Summary:**
Overview slide introducing voting rights, board composition, and protective provisions as the three pillars of control.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Three distinct control mechanisms
- Each can be negotiated independently
- Control terms are often as important as economic terms

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Which matters more to a VC: economic terms (liquidation preference, anti-dilution) or control terms (board, voting, protective provisions)? Does the answer depend on the stage of investment? Why might a VC accept worse economics in exchange for more control?"_

## SLIDE 150: Term Sheets — Control

**Content Summary:**
Voting rights, board composition, protective provisions. Certain actions cannot be taken without majority approval of a given share class.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Framework for control discussion
- Protective provisions = veto rights
- These are standard but heavily negotiated

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"What is the difference between voting rights and protective provisions? Give an example of something that would be covered by each. If you have voting rights on an as-converted basis, why would you also need protective provisions?"_

## SLIDE 151: Voting Rights

**Content Summary:**
Preferred holders vote as-if converted to common. Since each Series may have different timeframes and exit targets, different Series may not be economically aligned. New investor may want Series-level voting for more influence.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- As-if converted basis is standard
- Series-level voting = separate vote on matters affecting that specific series
- Prevents majority of one series from overriding minority of another

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"You have Series A (20%) and Series B (15%), founder owns 65%. But Series A has Series-level voting on key decisions. Does Series A have more power than their 20% suggests? At what point would Series A and Series B vote differently? Give a concrete example of a decision where their interests might conflict."_

---

## SLIDE 152: Board of Directors (BOD)

**Content Summary:**
BOD has formal duties — corporate governance. Includes duty of loyalty and care. One of the primary roles is fiduciary duty. New venture will grow and mature.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Legal framework underlying board decisions
- Fiduciary duty = must act in best interest of the company
- Even startup boards have legal obligations
- This is why board composition matters legally, not just economically

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Fiduciary duty means board members must act in the company's interest, not their own. But the VC board member was appointed by the investor. At what point might a VC-appointed board member face a conflict between their fiduciary duty and their obligation to their investor? Is this conflict real or theoretical?"_

---

## SLIDE 153: Board of Directors (BOD)

**Content Summary:**
Process of appointing BOD is key. Some PEs are terrible board members but good investors. BOD is the "inner sanctum, strategic planning department, and judge, jury and executioner, all in one."

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Memorable framing: board has multiple simultaneous roles
- Skeptical note: not all investors make good board members
- Finding balance between investor oversight and founder autonomy

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"'Judge, jury, and executioner' — what does this mean in practice? Give an example of a situation where the board exercises each of these roles. At what point does board oversight become board interference from the founder's perspective?"_

---

## SLIDE 154: Board of Directors (BOD)

**Content Summary:**
Balanced BOD: VC/PE has enough influence, founder/CEO has same number of seats, outside board member as good tie-breaker. More mature BOD: seven to nine members.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Typical early-stage structure: 2 founder seats, 1–2 investor seats, 1 independent
- Mature company: larger board with more independent directors
- Tie-breaker independent is valuable — can break deadlocks

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Design the board for an early-stage company: founder, Series A VC, Series B VC. What is the minimum structure that gives the founder operational control while giving investors appropriate oversight? How many seats? Who is the tie-breaker? Now design it from the VC's perspective — what minimum board representation would you require?"_

## SLIDE 155: Attributes of a High Quality Board Member

**Content Summary:**
Three-column table: Entrepreneurial (has started companies, hired A-players, knows customer discovery) | Domain Expertise (hands-on product building) | Sales/Customer (developed value proposition, sales pitch for new product).

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Three distinct attribute categories
- Different investors bring different attributes
- Board composition should balance these

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Rank the three attributes by importance for: (a) an early-stage consumer app, (b) a deep-tech hardware startup, (c) a B2B SaaS company. Does the most critical attribute change? Does a founder with strong entrepreneurial experience but weak domain expertise need a domain expert on the board? Or vice versa?"_

## SLIDE 156: Protective Provisions

**Content Summary:**
Effectively veto rights for key decisions. Allows VCs/PEs protection from material increase in business risks from management actions. Hotly debated: entrepreneur wants to give nothing, VCs want everything.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Veto over major decisions
- Standard list: new equity issuance, debt, asset sales, business sale, executive hires/fires
- Scope of veto is highly negotiated

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"List the 5 specific decisions that a VC would want veto rights over that you as a founder would agree to. Now list the 5 that a VC would want but you as founder would refuse. What is the difference between the two lists? What principle separates acceptable veto rights from unacceptable ones?"_

---

## SLIDE 157: Sale Restrictions — Drag-Along Right

**Content Summary:**
Gives a subset of investors the right to force all other investors and owners to agree to a sale. Critical for financial investors who need exit. Post-dotcom: VCs would rather control ability to compel deal.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Majority can force minority to sell
- Designed for liquidity — prevents minority from blocking exit
- After 2000 bubble: VCs learned they needed this more than they thought

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"You own 10% of a company. Majority investors (70%) find a buyer at what you believe is an undervalued price. Drag-along means you MUST sell. Is this fundamentally fair? What protections would a minority shareholder negotiate before agreeing to a drag-along clause? Design the minimum protection package."_

---

## SLIDE 158: Other Sale Restrictions

**Content Summary:**
Pre-emptive rights (pro-rata rights): VC can buy new shares on same terms as offered to others. First refusal rights: right to purchase shares proposed to be sold by any shareholder.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Pro-rata = right to participate in future rounds to maintain ownership
- First refusal = right to buy if another shareholder wants to sell
- Both protect investors from dilution and unwanted new shareholders

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"Series A investor owns 20%. Company raises Series B and offers Series A a pro-rata allocation at the same price as Series B. Does Series A HAVE to participate? What happens to their ownership percentage if they don't? Now: what if Series A doesn't have the cash to participate — does that change the answer?"_

---

## SLIDE 159: Redemption Rights

**Content Summary:**
Allows investors to require company to repurchase shares after specified period. Designed for when company is not an acquisition or IPO candidate. Prevents "lifestyle firms." Mandatory redemption = VC can "put" shares back to company.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Forces liquidity event
- Prevents company from being profitable but permanently private
- Can become a crisis if company can't afford redemption

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"VC invests $5M in Year 1, mandatory redemption at Year 7. By Year 6: company is profitable, $1M annual profit, but no acquisition offers, no IPO path. Company value estimated at $15M. What are the entrepreneur's options? Can the company afford to redeem at book value? What happens if they can't?"_

---

## SLIDE 160: Conversion

**Content Summary:**
Also known as registration rights. Stipulates preferred vs. common treatment in IPO. Most calculations use as-converted basis. Preferred can convert to common at any time.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Conversion right is always held by preferred shareholder
- Right to convert = right to become common for economic purposes
- Anti-dilution and liquidation preference both use as-converted basis for calculations

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"The VC has 1x participating preferred with full ratchet anti-dilution. At what point do they WANT to convert to common? At what point do they NOT want to convert? Is there an asymmetry in the decision — is the conversion option more valuable to the VC than to the common shareholder?"_

---

## SLIDE 161: Conversion (continued)

**Content Summary:**
Negotiation points: low multiple for mandatory conversion ($10–15M gross proceeds), gross vs. net proceeds. Threshold for mandatory/automatic conversion is critical.

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Low threshold = easier for entrepreneur to trigger mandatory conversion
- Gross proceeds = better for investor
- The threshold is a key negotiating point

**Engagement Line for Professor:**

> _"As a founder: would you negotiate for a high or low mandatory conversion threshold ($10M vs. $100M)? What does a high threshold protect you from? What does a low threshold enable? Now think from the VC's perspective: why might a VC want a LOW threshold for mandatory conversion?"_

## APPENDIX: Quick Reference Summary

### Liquidation Preference — Three Variants

| Structure               | Mechanism                          | VC Favors | Founder Favors |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------------- | --------- | -------------- |
| 1x Non-Participating    | Preference OR conversion           | No        | Yes            |
| 1x Participating        | Preference AND pro-rata            | Yes       | No             |
| 1x Participating Capped | Preference + participation, 2x cap | Moderate  | Moderate       |

### Anti-Dilution — Four Types

| Type                | Formula                         | Protection Level           |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| None                | —                               | No protection for investor |
| Broad Weighted Avg  | NCP = OCP × (OQ/NQ)             | Moderate                   |
| Narrow Weighted Avg | NCP = (OCP×OS + $New) / (OS+NS) | Strong                     |
| Full Ratchet        | NCP = new round price           | Maximum                    |

### Control Terms Summary

| Term                  | What It Controls             | Typical VC Ask              | Typical Founder Push     |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------- | --------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Voting Rights         | Board seats, major decisions | As-converted + Series level | Common only              |
| Board Composition     | BOD makeup                   | 1–2 seats + influence       | Founder majority         |
| Protective Provisions | Veto on key actions          | Broad list                  | Narrow, operational only |
| Drag-Along            | Forced sale                  | Low threshold               | Price floor + tag-along  |
| Redemption            | Forced buyback               | 5–7 year mandatory          | None preferred           |
| Pre-emptive Rights    | Future rounds                | Pro-rata participation      | Willing to share         |
