## SECTION 1: Liquidation Preference (Slides 128–137)

### Slide 128 — Liquidation Preference: Core Concept

**What it says:**

- Two components: preference + participation
- Preference = actual claims before other classes
- States a multiple of original investment returned before common gets anything

**Engagement Line:**

> _If you invested $2M for 20% of a company at a $10M valuation, and the company sells for $3M — as a preferred shareholder with 1x preference, do you get your full $2M back? What happens to the remaining $1M? Draw it out on a timeline._

---

### Slide 129 — Types: Non-Participating Preference

**What it says:**

- Most basic form — also called "simple liquidation preference"
- Entitles holder to pre-determined portion = amount originally invested
- Either takes preference payout OR converts to common — not both

**Engagement Line:**

> _As an entrepreneur: would you prefer a non-participating preference or participating? As an investor? What are the trade-offs?_

---

### Slides 130–135 — Liquidation Preference Exercises & Participation Types

**1x Preference, Non-Participating (Slide 132)**

- Investor gets 1x or common equivalent — whichever is greater
- Exit at $5M: investor takes 1x ($2M), entrepreneur gets $3M
- Exit at $500K: investor takes 1x ($2M), entrepreneur gets nothing

**1x Preference, Participating (Slide 133)**

- Investor gets 1x preference AND shares remaining pro-rata
- Double-dip: gets payout twice

**1x Preference, Participating Capped at 2x (Slide 134)**

- Investor participates but only up to 2x total return
- Caps upside, limits entrepreneur dilution

**Engagement Line:**

> _Scenario: VC invests $10M for 50% of company at $20M pre-money ($20M post-money). Exit at $50M. Calculate investor return under: (a) non-participating, (b) participating, (c) participating capped at 2x. Which would the entrepreneur prefer? Which would the VC prefer?_

---

### Slide 136 — Multiple Series (Stacked Preferences)

**What it says:**

- Series B stacks on top of Series A
- Two approaches: stacked preferences vs. pari passu (equal ranking)
- Negotiation point

**Engagement Line:**

> _If you're Series B investor joining a company with an existing Series A that has 1x non-participating preference — would you want your preference stacked above or pari passu with Series A? Why might the entrepreneur push for pari passu?_

---

### Slide 137 — Focus on Value Not Valuation

**What it says:**

- High valuation pursuit can be counter-productive
- Liquidation preference level signals VC's view of current valuation vs. future potential
- Preference = insurance against poor performance

**Engagement Line:**

> _Professor's insight: "A VC demanding high liquidation preference is basically saying 'I think this valuation is too rich.'" How does this change how you negotiate? Does a high pre-money valuation with high preference actually benefit the entrepreneur?_

## SECTION 2: Anti-Dilution (Slides 138–147)

### Slide 138–139 — Anti-Dilution Concept

**What it says:**

- Protects investors when firm issues equity at lower valuation (down-round)
- Two scenarios: economic impact + control impact
- Firms may forgo lower-valuation financing to avoid anti-dilution

**Engagement Line:**

> _You raised Series A at $3/share. Now market conditions force you to raise Series B at $2/share. As Series A investor with NO anti-dilution, what happened to your stake? With anti-dilution, what should happen?_

---

### Slide 140 — Full Ratchet Adjustment

**What it says:**

- Conversion price adjusted fully to new (lower) investor's price
- Most investor-friendly form
- Series A investor who paid $3/share → adjusted to $2/share (Series B price)

**Engagement Line:**

> _Full ratchet is considered very aggressive against founders. Why might a VC insist on it? Under what market conditions would a founder agree to it?_

---

### Slide 141 — Weighted Average: Narrow

**Formula:** NCP = (OCP × OS + NSM) / (OS + NS)

Where:

- NCP = New Conversion Price
- OCP = Original Conversion Price ($3)
- OS = Original Shares
- NS = New Shares issued at lower price
- NSM = New Shares × new price (dollar value)

**Engagement Line:**

> _Walk through the numbers: Series A at $3/share = 3M shares ($9M). Series B at $2/share raises $2M (1M new shares). What is the new conversion price for Series A under narrow weighted average? How many extra shares does Series A investor receive?_

---

### Slide 142 — Anti-Dilution Example Walkthrough

**What it says:**

- $9M invested at $3/share = 3M shares
- With weighted average dilution: each original share priced at $2.66
- Investor gets ~3.38M shares (0.38M free shares)
- Neutralizes negative wealth impact

**Engagement Line:**

> _Verify the math: NCP = ($3 × 3M + $2M) / (3M + 1M) = $11M / 4M = $2.75. But the slide says $2.66. Why the difference? (Hint: check the NSM term — it might be just the dollar amount raised, not NS×price)._

---

### Slide 143 — Weighted Average: Broad

**Formula:** NCP = OCP × (OQ / NQ)

- OQ = Original Qualified shares (fully diluted)
- NQ = New Qualified shares (fully diluted after new round)

**Engagement Line:**

> _Broad-based uses fully diluted share count (including all options, warrants, reserved shares). Narrow uses only actual shares issued. Which protects the investor more? Which protects the entrepreneur more?_

---

### Slides 144–147 — Anti-Dilution Exercises

**Engagement Line:**

> _Work through each exercise table. For each scenario: calculate (a) conversion price before, (b) shares before, (c) shares after anti-dilution, (d) new % owned. Then ask: did the investor's stake increase, decrease, or stay the same in each case?_

## SECTION 3: Pay-To-Play (Slide 148)

**What it says:**

- Penalizes investors who don't participate in future financing rounds
- Non-participating investors lose preferential rights if they don't "stand up" and support future rounds
- Encourages investors to back ventures in down-round situations
- Useful for struggling ventures needing follow-on financing

**Engagement Line:**

> _As an investor: would you prefer (a) standard non-participating preference with no PTP, or (b) same preference but with PTP clause? What is the implicit deal here — the investor is essentially committing to what in exchange for what?_

## SECTION 4: Key Control Terms (Slides 149–161)

### Slide 150 — Control Terms Overview

**What it says:**

- Voting rights
- Board composition
- Protective provisions

**Engagement Line:**

> _From a founder's perspective, which control term matters most and why? From an investor's perspective?_

---

### Slide 151 — Voting Rights

**What it says:**

- Preferred holders vote as-if converted to common
- Straightforward — rarely debated
- Series-level voting: new investors may want separate Series vote for more influence

**Engagement Line:**

> _If you have Series A (20%) and Series B (15%) and a founder (65%), but Series A has Series-level voting on key decisions — what actually happens? Does Series A have more power than their ownership suggests?_

---

### Slide 152–154 — Board of Directors

**What it says:**

- BOD has fiduciary duties (duty of loyalty + care)
- Balanced BOD: VC/PE has enough influence, Founder/CEO has same number of seats, outside member as tie-breaker
- Mature BOD: 7–9 members

**Engagement Line:**

> _"Some PEs are terrible board members but good investors." Have you seen examples of this? When you design a board, what is the minimum composition you would accept as a founder? What would an investor insist on?_

---

### Slide 155 — Attributes of a High Quality Board Member

Three columns: Entrepreneurial, Domain Expertise, Sales/Customer

**Engagement Line:**

> _Rank the three attributes for (a) an early-stage consumer startup, (b) a deep-tech hardware venture, (c) a SaaS B2B company. Does the most important attribute change?_

---

### Slide 156 — Protective Provisions

**What it says:**

- Veto rights for key decisions
- Hotly debated: entrepreneur wants to give nothing, investor wants everything
- Protects investors from material increase in business risk

**Engagement Line:**

> _What specific actions should require investor approval? Make a list of 5 "negative covenants" that protect investors without crippling the entrepreneur's ability to run the business. Then cross out the 2 you think most entrepreneurs would resist most fiercely._

---

### Slide 157 — Drag-Along Right

**What it says:**

- Subset of investors can force ALL shareholders to agree to a sale
- Critical for financial investors who need exit
- Post-dotcom: VCs would rather control ability to compel deal than get stuck with illiquid holding

**Engagement Line:**

> _You own 10% of a company. Majority investors (70%) find a buyer at a price you think undervalues the company. Drag-along means you MUST sell. Is this fair? What protections could you negotiate (e.g., minimum price floor, tag-along right to join the buyer's cap table)?_

---

### Slide 158 — Pre-Emptive Rights & First Refusal Rights

**What it says:**

- Pre-emptive rights: VC can buy new shares on same terms offered to others (pro-rata future financing)
- First refusal rights: right to purchase shares proposed to be sold by any shareholder

**Engagement Line:**

> _As Series A investor with pre-emptive rights: you own 20%. Company raises Series B — you are offered pro-rata. But Series B is at a higher valuation, so participating costs more. Do you have to participate? What happens to your ownership % if you don't?_

---

### Slide 159 — Redemption Rights

**What it says:**

- Allows investors to require company to repurchase shares after specified period
- Designed for when company is neither acquisition nor IPO candidate
- Prevents ventures from becoming "lifestyle firms"
- Puts pressure on entrepreneur to create liquidity event

**Engagement Line:**

> _VC invests $5M in Year 1 with mandatory redemption at Year 7. By Year 6, company is profitable but private — no IPO, no acquisition prospects. What are the entrepreneur's options? What happens if the company can't afford to redeem at the specified price?_

---

### Slides 160–161 — Conversion Rights

**What it says:**

- Stipulates preferred vs. common treatment in IPO
- Most calculations use as-converted basis
- Preferred can convert to common at any time
- Key thresholds: low multiple ($10-15M gross proceeds), mandatory conversion trigger

**Engagement Line:**

> _As a founder: would you negotiate for a high or low mandatory conversion threshold ($10M vs. $100M)? How does this affect your control at different exit sizes?_

## Quick Reference: Key Formulas

| Concept                               | Formula                                         |
| ------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| **Narrow Weighted Avg Anti-Dilution** | NCP = (OCP × OS + $ New Investment) / (OS + NS) |
| **Broad Weighted Avg Anti-Dilution**  | NCP = OCP × (OQ / NQ)                           |
| **Non-Participating Liquidation**     | Min(preference × investment, pro-rata share)    |
| **Participating Liquidation**         | Preference payout + (remaining × ownership %)   |

## Common VC/PE Positions vs. Founder Positions

| Term                   | VC/PE Preference         | Founder Preference            |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------ | ----------------------------- |
| Liquidation Preference | High (2x, participating) | Low (1x, non-participating)   |
| Anti-Dilution          | Full ratchet             | Broad weighted average        |
| Board Composition      | Majority seats           | Minority with tie-breaker     |
| Drag-Along Threshold   | Low (majority can force) | High (supermajority required) |
| Redemption             | Yes, 5–7 year mandatory  | No redemption preferred       |
