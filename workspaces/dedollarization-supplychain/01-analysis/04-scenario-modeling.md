# Scenario Modeling: 10%, 30%, 50% Dedollarized World

## Framework

Dedollarization is not binary. It is a spectrum of displacement across three dimensions:

1. **Trade settlement currency** — what currencies clear transactions
2. **Reserve composition** — what assets central banks hold
3. **FX market microstructure** — what currencies anchor the global monetary system

---

## Scenario A: 10% Dedollarized World

**Description**: Slow erosion — current trajectory maintained through 2030.

**What Changes**:

- Dollar share of global FX reserves falls from 57% → ~51%
- China Treasury holdings decline to ~$400B
- mBridge transaction volume reaches ~$200B (from ~$1.1B today — BIS conservative figure; Coalition self-report claims $55B but methodology unclear)
- Gold becomes 12-15% of global central bank reserves (from ~10% currently)
- Petrodollar still intact; no oil pricing in non-dollar currencies
- BRICS Pay: not operational as unified system

**What Doesn't Change**:

- Dollar remains dominant in oil pricing
- SWIFT handles >90% of cross-border payments
- US borrowing costs unchanged
- Dollar still ~80% of global FX market transactions (microstructure)

**Likelihood**: ~80% (base case — red team corrected from 65%)

---

## Scenario B: 30% Dedollarized World

**Description**: Structural break — a catalyst accelerates the trend.

**Catalysts That Could Trigger This**:

- A major geopolitical event (Taiwan conflict, Saudi Arabia fully exits petrodollar, Russia-China trade fully dedollarized)
- A credible replacement reserve currency emerges (most likely: a dollar-euro-yuan hybrid or SDR-lite)
- US fiscal crisis forces mass Treasury selling by foreign holders

**What Changes**:

- Dollar FX reserve share: ~40-45%
- China Treasury holdings: sub-$300B; GCC holdings significantly reduced
- mBridge becomes primary settlement rail for Asia-Middle East trade
- Gold reserves: 18-22% of global central bank reserves
- 20-30% of global oil trade settles in non-dollar currencies
- CIPS + mBridge integration creates credible RMB settlement corridor to Middle East
- US Treasury yields rise 50-100bps as foreign demand weakens

**What Doesn't Change**:

- Dollar remains #1 reserve currency
- SWIFT still handles dollar-denominated transactions globally
- No single alternative displaces dollar; multipolar currency system emerges
- US capital markets remain deepest in world

**Likelihood**: ~17% (realistic within 5-7 years if catalyst emerges — red team corrected from 25%)

---

## Scenario C: 50%+ Dedollarized World

**Description**: Systemic regime change — the dollar loses reserve currency primacy.

**This Would Require**:

- Collapse of US fiscal credibility OR emergence of a genuine multilateral reserve alternative
- Most likely trigger: a US debt crisis that spooks foreign Treasury holders into coordinated selling
- Secondary trigger: geopolitical realignment where US security guarantees to Gulf collapse

**What Changes**:

- Dollar FX reserve share: 30-35%
- US borrowing costs spike 200-400bps; dollar loses 30-40% of its value
- mBridge + CIPS + BRICS Pay integration creates a parallel global payment system
- Gold becomes 25-30% of reserves globally
- Global financial architecture splits into two blocs (dollar-West vs. yuan/BRICS East)
- Oil pricing in dollars becomes contested; petroyuan/petroeuro hybrid emerges
- IMF SDR becomes central reserve asset

**What Doesn't Change**:

- Dollar still major currency (losing reserve status ≠ losing trade currency status)
- US remains largest economy in PPP terms
- Dollar remains dominant in capital markets for decades even if FX share declines

**Likelihood**: ~3% (5-10 year timeframe if US fiscal trajectory continues unchecked — red team corrected from 10%)

---

## Critical Indicators to Watch

**Leading Indicators** (months ahead):

- [ ] China Treasury holdings falling below $500B
- [ ] mBridge transaction volume exceeding $500B
- [ ] Dollar FX reserve share below 52%
- [ ] Any G20 central bank adding >100 tonnes gold/month for 3+ consecutive months

**Lag Indicators** (years):

- [ ] Dollar FX share below 50%
- [ ] Oil trade in non-dollar currencies exceeding 20%
- [ ] BRICS unified payment system operational
- [ ] US 10-year Treasury yield consistently above 5.5% (indicating demand destruction)

---

## The Phase Transition Question

The dollar system has **tipping point dynamics**. Once a threshold is crossed — say, dollar reserve share below 45% — the self-reinforcing logic of reserve currency status begins to work in reverse.

Currently: dollar dominance → foreign demand for Treasuries → low US borrowing costs → easy fiscal policy → dollar dominance

The reverse spiral (if triggered):
foreign selling → higher yields → fiscal pressure → weaker dollar → faster foreign selling

**This is not happening today** — but the question is whether the 2026-2030 window creates the conditions for a regime transition.
