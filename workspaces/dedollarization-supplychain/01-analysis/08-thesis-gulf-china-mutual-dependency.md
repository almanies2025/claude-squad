# Thesis 4: Gulf-China Mutual Dependency as Dedollarization Engine

**Date**: 2026-04-23
**Status**: Red-teamed — 25/50, structural logic sound, empirical support mixed

---

## Core Thesis

Gulf countries are building dedollarization infrastructure because China is their primary trade customer _and_ their primary supplier of processed critical minerals. US financial, technology, and military industrial complexes cannot oppose this because cutting off Gulf access to Chinese minerals would cut off US access to processed rare earths — collapsing US defense and tech production. Mutual dependency is the structural lock.

**Key distinguishing claim**: No coordination required. No "US managing transition." No "FIC building post-dollar infrastructure." The mechanism is purely structural trade dependency — each actor maximizes its own interest, and dedollarization emerges from the structure.

---

## The Mechanism

```
China = Gulf's largest customer (oil, re-exports, resources)
China = Gulf's critical mineral supplier (processed REEs, cobalt, gallium)
         ↓
USD settlement = unnecessary cost + political risk (SWIFT sanctions exposure)
         ↓
Gulf builds: mBridge + bilateral currency swaps + CBDC rails + CNY oil pricing
         ↓
US cannot sanction without self-harm (rare earth supply chain disruption)
         ↓
US tacit acceptance → dedollarization proceeds
```

---

## What the Evidence Shows

### China-Gulf Trade Dependency (Confirmed)

- Saudi Arabia: Primary oil customer is China (volume growing, US volume stable/declining)
- UAE: Major re-export hub connecting China to Middle East and Africa
- Gulf-China bilateral trade: Growing as share of total Gulf trade
- Belt and Road: Physical infrastructure connecting Gulf to Chinese supply chains

### China Rare Earth Processing Dominance (Confirmed)

| Mineral                   | China's Processing Share | Assessment              |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------ | ----------------------- |
| Rare earth elements (REE) | ~85-90%                  | Dominant                |
| Gallium                   | ~80%                     | Dominant                |
| Germanium                 | ~60%                     | Dominant                |
| Cobalt refining           | ~65%                     | Dominant (ore from DRC) |
| Lithium processing        | ~60%                     | Dominant                |
| Aluminum                  | ~57%                     | Dominant                |
| Copper                    | ~50%                     | Significant             |

**Key insight**: China's dominance is in _processing_, not mining. Ore from DRC, Australia, and South America flows through Chinese refineries. US Geological Survey: China supplies ~75-80% of US rare earth imports — processed compounds, not raw ore.

### US Defense/Tech Rare Earth Dependency (Confirmed)

- F-35: ~417 kg rare earth materials per unit
- Virginia-class submarine: significant rare earths for motors/electronics
- Semiconductor production: gallium nitride (GaN), germanium substrates — China-dominated
- Missile systems, EVs, wind turbines: similar dependency

**Constraint realism**: Not "collapse overnight" but "7-15 year timeline to build alternative processing." Economic damage and timeline expansion, not instant break.

### US Not Applying Maximum Pressure (Confirmed)

- No secondary sanctions on Saudi Arabia or UAE for mBridge participation
- No OFAC designations for mBridge participation
- US Treasury issued advisories but no enforcement actions
- BIS mBridge project: US did not vote to block UAE/Saudi participation

### Gulf USD Pegs Maintained (Contradiction)

- Saudi Riyal (SAR): pegged at 3.75 per USD
- UAE Dirham (AED): pegged at 3.67 per USD

**The fundamental contradiction**: A country on a USD peg cannot simultaneously lead structural dedollarization without abandoning the peg. The thesis does not resolve this.

---

## What Remains Unresolved

### The USD Peg Problem

The thesis never explains how a country on a USD peg can simultaneously reduce dollar dependency. Possible resolutions:

1. **Dual-track system**: Most Gulf trade settles USD via the peg; only China-bilateral trade uses alternatives. This is trade facilitation, not dedollarization.

2. **Building options, not replacing**: Gulf is building dedollarization capacity for extremis scenarios — a hedge, not an active transition.

3. **Pegs are transitional**: Infrastructure built now, pegs will break when alternatives are operationally ready.

### De-Westernization vs. Dedollarization

mBridge is BIS-hosted, multi-central-bank infrastructure — not China-controlled. This is better characterized as **de-Westernization** (alternatives to Western-controlled SWIFT/CHIPS) than **dedollarization** (dollar replaced as reserve currency).

CNY oil pricing between China and Gulf is bilateral. It does not displace the dollar from its reserve currency role.

### US Alternative Supply Chains In Development

IRA + BIL: $450M+ committed to domestic rare earth processing. MP Materials (Mountain Pass, California): operational refining. Lynas (Australia): under US DoD contract. This is a transitional state, not a static dependency.

---

## Falsification Conditions

**1. US Alternative Supply Chains >40% Operational by 2030**

- Observable: MP Materials output >10,000 tonnes/year REO equivalent; Lynas deliveries to US defense contractors; DOE strategic stockpile expansions

**2. US Applies Credible Secondary Sanctions on Gulf mBridge Participants**

- Observable: OFAC designation of Saudi/UAE entity for mBridge participation; Swift removal threat; Congressional testimony naming mBridge as sanctions target

**3. Gulf States Break or Abandon USD Pegs**

- Observable: SAR or AED announces floating or currency basket; central bank reserve composition shows significant USD reduction; explicit statement from Saudi/UAE monetary authority

**4. Gulf-China Trade Share Reverses**

- Observable: 3+ consecutive years of declining China share of Gulf trade; Saudi General Authority for Statistics data; UAE customs data

**5. mBridge Remains Marginal (<$5B, No Expansion)**

- Observable: BIS dashboard shows <$5B cumulative; no new Gulf joiners beyond UAE/Saudi; no commercial bank adoption

**What the thesis gets right**: Gulf-China trade dependency is real and structural. China dominates critical mineral processing. US-Gulf alignment is under stress. The mechanism is directionally correct even if specifics are wrong.

**What the thesis gets wrong**: mBridge volumes are marginal ($1.1B BIS conservative vs. trillions in Gulf-US trade). "US cannot act" is too sweeping (US has chosen not to act for strategic reasons). US is building alternatives (IRA/BIL). USD pegs are structurally incompatible with active dedollarization.

**The core insight worth preserving**: The rare earth processing dependency creates a genuine structural constraint on US Gulf policy. This is real. The thesis overstates how far that constraint has already translated into dedollarization.

## Key Question for Further Research

**If the thesis is correct that mBridge is primarily de-Westernization (not dedollarization), what does this mean for the scenario model probabilities?**

- De-Westernization can proceed while dollar retains reserve status
- Bilateral CNY pricing can grow without displacing dollar from core roles
- The scenario where dollar _keeps_ reserve status but _loses_ SWIFT dominance is distinct from the scenario where dollar loses reserve status entirely

These may need to be separated in the scenario model.
