## Executive Summary

The dedollarization analysis makes a defensible case for "slow erosion toward multipolar monetary system" but systematically overweights evidence supporting the thesis while underweighting or dismissing contradictory signals. The report correctly identifies infrastructure developments (mBridge, CIPS) and structural trends (China's Treasury selling, gold accumulation) but commits several critical analytical errors: treating intentions as outcomes, conflating infrastructure build with adoption, and citing weak sources to reinforce strong claims. The 10/30/50% scenario framework is reasonable but poorly calibrated, with the base case (65% probability for 10% dedollarization) essentially describing the status quo rather than a meaningful dedollarization scenario.

**Bottom Line**: The thesis is directionally correct but overstated in pace and confidence. The most likely outcome is continued slow erosion, NOT the "phase transition" the report leaves open as a plausible scenario.

**Cherry-Pick #2: Gold Demand Framing**

The report emphasizes the 2022-2024 record (>1,000 tonnes/year) but then admits 2025 saw only ~630-860 tonnes — a 21-37% decline from the peak. The WGC survey data (43% plan to increase) measures intentions, not behavior, and intentions often diverge from outcomes.

**Cherry-Pick #3: mBridge Volume Framing**

The 2,500x growth since 2022 is impressive-sounding but starting from near-zero. $55B cumulative volume against $100T+ in annual global trade is 0.055%. The report acknowledges this context but then immediately pivots to growth rate framing — classic selection bias.

## Section 2: Counterfactual Evidence

### 2.1 Evidence Contradicting the Thesis

**Counterfactual #1: Dollar Was Supposed to Collapse After 2008 — It Didn't**

The 2008 financial crisis was widely predicted to accelerate dedollarization. It didn't. The dollar strengthened. The same predictions followed QE, Afghanistan, Iraq, and every US fiscal concern. The dedollarization thesis has been "imminent" for 20 years.

**Counterfactual #2: Yuan Internationalization Has Stalled**

China's capital controls remain in place. Yuan's share of global FX reserves is ~2-3%, not the 10-15% predicted by models in 2015-2016 when CIPS launched. The trade surplus thesis (more RMB outflows = more internationalization) ignores that surplus countries typically don't have reserve currency status — they accumulate reserves, not distribute them.

**Counterfactual #3: BRICS Has Made Zero Progress on Unified Currency in 15 Years**

The BRICS has existed since 2009. At the 2024 Kazan summit, BRICS Pay was still described as a "framework" — no unified system, no common settlement asset, no consensus on a common denominator. The EU took 50 years to build the euro. BRICS has been trying for 15 and has nothing to show.

**Counterfactual #4: GCC States Are Issuing Record Dollar Debt**

The report acknowledges this paradox but doesn't adequately explain it. If GCC states genuinely believed in structural dedollarization, they would be reducing dollar liabilities, not increasing them. Record GCC dollar debt issuance ($55B in 2025) is strong revealed preference against the thesis.

**Counterfactual #5: Gold Demand Is Actually Slowing**

2025 gold purchases (~630-860 tonnes) were significantly below the 2022-2024 peak (>1,000 tonnes/year). If central banks were in a structural gold accumulation mode, why did purchases decline? The report attributes this to price deterrence but doesn't consider that the "structural" thesis might be wrong.

### 2.2 What the Analysis Missed or Underweighted

**Underweighted: The Dollar Weaponization Paradox Cuts Both Ways**

The report correctly identifies that SWIFT weaponization creates incentives for alternatives. But it ignores: (a) US has shown willingness to use secondary sanctions to enforce dollar dominance, (b) countries cut off from SWIFT still struggle to find viable alternatives, (c) the US can and does compel third-country banks to comply via the "secondary sanctions" mechanism.

**Underweighted: Domestic Constraints in Key Countries**

China's capital account is not fully convertible. This is the fundamental constraint on RMB internationalization. You cannot have a global reserve currency without capital account convertibility. China has shown no willingness to liberalize this — and liberalizing would threaten the Communist Party's domestic control.

**Underweighted: Euro Is Losing Reserve Share Faster Than Dollar**

Since 2022, euro's reserve share has declined more rapidly than dollar's share. If dedollarization were real, we would expect to see diversification INTO euro and yuan, not just OUT of dollar. Instead, the dollar's relative share is stable — it's being replaced by gold and small currencies, not by a successor reserve currency.

**Underweighted: Dollar Dominance in Capital Markets Is Different From FX Reserves**

The analysis conflates FX reserve composition with capital market dominance. Dollar-denominated bonds remain the global safe haven. When crises hit, capital flows INTO dollar assets, not out. The 2022 Ukraine invasion triggered massive dollar strengthening, not weakness. The 2024 banking stress saw dollar appreciation. This is the opposite of what dedollarization theory predicts.

## Section 3: Assumption Audit

### 3.1 Critical Implicit Assumptions

**Assumption #1: Infrastructure Build Equals Adoption**

The analysis assumes mBridge, CIPS, and SPFS will gradually gain adoption because they exist. This ignores:

- Network effects strongly favor incumbent (SWIFT processed $100T+ in 2024)
- Adoption requires trust in the new system — which doesn't exist yet
- Users need confidence in dispute resolution, regulatory oversight, continuity
- The analysis nowhere addresses switching costs or integration complexity

**Assumption #2: Saudi Arabia's "Decision" Was Real and Permanent**

The Fortune article (April 2026) claims Saudi Arabia cancelled a "petrodollar arrangement" that was "2 years old" — meaning the decision was made around April 2024, during a period of US-China tensions. But:

- No official Saudi confirmation exists
- Saudi Arabia simultaneously issued record dollar debt
- Saudi Arabia has not publicly confirmed any change to oil pricing
- The US-Saudi relationship has survived many tensions before

**Assumption #3: Gold Accumulation Is Anti-Dollar**

The analysis treats gold buying as evidence of dedollarization. But:

- Gold can be accumulated as a diversifier, not a replacement
- Central banks have been reducing gold since 2010 after selling heavily in 1990s-2000s
- Gold is also a political hedge against US sanctions — not exclusively anti-dollar
- Some gold buyers (Poland, Kazakhstan) have specific domestic political reasons

**Assumption #4: The 2026-2030 Window Is Structurally Distinct**

The analysis claims "2026-2030 is the critical observation period." But this could have been said in 2016, 2021, or 2031. There's no specific mechanism identified that makes 2026-2030 uniquely decisive.

**Assumption #5: Dollar FX Reserve Share Is the Right Metric**

The report obsesses over FX reserve share (72% peak to 57% current) but:

- This measures central bank holdings, not private market behavior
- Private markets have increased dollar exposure in many periods
- The "dollar's share of new accumulation" is more relevant but harder to measure
- The dollar's role in FX spot trading is more durable than reserve share

### 3.2 Assumption Interdependency

If ANY of these assumptions fail, the thesis weakens substantially:

| Assumption                       | If Wrong                                                    |
| -------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Saudi "cancellation" is real     | The structural break disappears; petrodollar remains intact |
| mBridge adoption accelerates     | Infrastructure sits unused; no network effect               |
| China continues Treasury selling | China's constraints prevent full exit; rebound continues    |
| Gold accumulation is structural  | 2025 decline was not a correction but the trend             |
| Capital controls can be bypassed | RMB internationalization hits the hard ceiling              |

## Section 4: Alternative Explanations

### 4.1 Cyclical Explanations for Observed Trends

**Alternative #1: The Trends Are Cyclical, Not Structural**

China's Treasury selling could be explained by:

- Need to prop up yuan during capital outflows (not policy choice)
- Domestic liquidity management during property crisis
- BP前回购置美元资产到期不续 (non-renewal of maturing holdings)

Gold accumulation could be explained by:

- Post-2022 geopolitical uncertainty driving safe-haven demand
- Diversification from negative-yielding bonds (not from dollars specifically)
- Zakat requirements for Muslim central banks (not anti-dollar)

**Alternative #2: US Policy Choices, Not Structural Forces**

The "dollar weaponization paradox" (every sanction accelerates alternatives) is real — but US policy also demonstrates:

- Willingness to provide dollar liquidity during crises (2020, 2023)
- Fed swap lines that strengthen dollar in crises
- Strategic use of secondary sanctions that extend dollar reach

**Alternative #3: The Data Reflects Measurement Problems**

FX reserve composition data (COFER) has a ~18-month lag. The 56.9% figure could reflect data from mid-2024 — before the dollar strengthened significantly in late 2024-2025. This would explain why the reported figure seems inconsistent with contemporaneous dollar strength.

### 4.2 Why Alternative Explanations Are Plausible

- Every major "dedollarization" prediction since 2008 has proven premature
- The structural change the thesis requires (unified BRICS alternative, Saudi oil in non-dollar) hasn't happened in 50 years of dollar dominance
- Network effects in global finance are extremely powerful and slow-moving
- The report's own evidence (mBridge 0.2% of trade, yuan <3% reserves) is more consistent with the alternative hypothesis

## Section 5: Structural vs. Cyclical Assessment

### 5.1 What Would Prove It's Structural?

For the dedollarization thesis to be proven structural (not cyclical), the following must be demonstrated:

1. **Consistent directional change across multiple independent metrics** — The report shows this partially, but gold buying declined in 2025, China rebounded in Feb 2026, and GCC is issuing dollar debt

2. **Absence of reversal patterns** — Dollar has reversed every dedollarization trend within 5-7 years since 1971

3. **Causal mechanism identified** — "US weaponization of SWIFT" explains why countries build alternatives, but doesn't explain why they would succeed this time when they failed after 2008, Iran sanctions (2012), etc.

4. **No credible alternative exists** — The report acknowledges this but argues it doesn't matter because "bypass not replacement" — but bypass at scale requires the same network effects as replacement

### 5.2 Evidence FOR Cyclical Interpretation

| Indicator               | Structural Reading      | Cyclical Reading                               |
| ----------------------- | ----------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| China Treasury selling  | Policy-driven exit      | Capital flow management during property crisis |
| Gold accumulation       | Reserve diversification | Geopolitical safe-haven demand                 |
| mBridge volume          | Early-stage adoption    | Pilot projects not scaling                     |
| Dollar FX share decline | Secular trend           | Measurement lag + euro weakness                |
| Saudi "pivot"           | Structural break        | Diplomatic positioning, no policy change       |

**Assessment: The cyclical interpretation is MORE consistent with the data.** The report systematically chooses the structural reading when both are plausible.

## Section 6: Scenario Modeling Weaknesses

### 6.1 Scenario Architecture Problems

**Problem #1: 10% Dedollarization = Status Quo**

The "base case" scenario describes a world where the dollar's FX share falls from 57% to ~51% — which is the continuation of the existing 24-year trend. This is not a dedollarization scenario; it is the default scenario. Calling 10% dedollarization a 65% probability outcome when the trend has been 10% dedollarization per 15 years is tautological.

**Problem #2: No Scenario for Dollar Strengthening**

The framework has no scenario where:

- Dollar strengthens significantly
- Yuan internationalization stalls due to capital controls
- BRICS cooperation fails further
- US fiscal consolidation occurs

This creates a built-in bias toward dedollarization outcomes.

**Problem #3: The 50% Scenario Requires US Fiscal Crisis**

The most extreme scenario (dollar loses reserve primacy) requires either:

- US debt crisis (plausible but not base case)
- Emergence of credible alternative (not in 5-year window)

The analysis gives this 10% probability — which seems reasonable but the scenario is internally inconsistent: it assumes US fiscal credibility collapses while simultaneously assuming BRICS builds unified infrastructure.

**Problem #4: Missing Scenario — Gradual Convergence**

What if dedollarization proceeds exactly as it has for 50 years (slow, partial, not destabilizing)? This is the most likely scenario and the one the analysis should focus on.

### 6.2 Probability Calibration Issues

The probability distribution (65/25/10) is not derived from a model. It appears to be expert judgment — which is appropriate — but the report doesn't reveal:

- What indicators would shift probability toward B or C
- What the counterfactual base rate is (dedollarization predictions have ~0% accuracy historically)
- How the scenario probabilities relate to specific catalyst events

A better framing: "Dedollarization has been predicted to accelerate repeatedly since 1971. Each cycle of prediction has proven premature. Our 65% base case assumes continued gradualism."

## Section 7: BRICS Capacity Assessment

### 7.1 BRICS Overweighted in Analysis

**GDP PPP vs. Nominal GDP**: BRICS PPP GDP of ~$60T sounds impressive but PPP overstates EM purchasing power. Using nominal GDP, BRICS is ~$25-30T — less than the US alone. The report uses PPP figures throughout, inflating BRICS economic weight.

**Internal Contradictions Are Fatal**: The analysis correctly identifies that BRICS members have incompatible monetary interests but then treats the coalition as a potential alternative. A coalition that cannot agree on a common currency cannot build a unified payment system. This is not a technical problem; it's a political economy problem.

**mBridge Is Not BRICS Infrastructure**: The report treats mBridge as BRICS infrastructure when it is actually a BIS-China-UAE initiative. Russia (a BRICS member) is not on mBridge. Saudi Arabia (BRICS member) is on mBridge but also maintains dollar peg. The analysis conflates these.

### 7.2 What BRICS Can and Cannot Do

**CAN DO** (already doing):

- Bilateral dedollarization corridors between aligned pairs (China-Russia: done)
- mBridge for Asia-Gulf settlement
- Political narrative around dedollarization
- Gold accumulation by individual members

**CANNOT DO** (in any credible timeframe):

- Unified BRICS payment system (zero progress in 15 years)
- Common reserve currency (no consensus possible given internal conflicts)
- Global infrastructure to rival SWIFT/IMF/BIS
- Break dollar oil pricing (Saudi has not moved; no replacement exists)

## Section 8: Missing Variables

### 8.1 Critical Variables Absent from Analysis

**Variable #1: US Fiscal Trajectory**

The analysis mentions US fiscal deficits as a catalyst but doesn't model them as a base case variable. If US fiscal position deteriorates to the point where Treasury yields spike, this would accelerate dedollarization AND constitute a US self-inflicted wound. The 2025 US fiscal position (deficit ~6-7% of GDP) is already concerning.

**Variable #2: Technology — Digital Dollar and FedNow**

The analysis ignores US responses to dedollarization threats:

- FedNow (instant payment system) launched 2023
- Potential digital dollar could entrench dollar dominance in new ways
- US has not attempted to counter dedollarization with positive incentives

**Variable #3: European Monetary Policy**

Euro's trajectory is critical. If euro continues losing reserve share, the multipolar system the analysis envisions has no second pillar. The EU's fiscal constraints and political fragmentation limit euro's potential. The analysis treats euro as stable and focuses only on dollar-yuan dynamics.

**Variable #4: Capital Account Convertibility**

The analysis never mentions China's capital controls as a binding constraint. RMB internationalization requires either:

- Full capital account convertibility (which China will not accept), OR
- A closed system where RMB circulates only within China's sphere of influence

The second option means RMB internationalization caps out at China-centric trade corridors — not global reserve currency status.

**Variable #5: Next Global Crisis Response**

In every global crisis since 2008, dollar has strengthened. This is because:

- Dollar-denominated assets are the global safe haven
- Fed swap lines provide dollar liquidity to allied central banks
- US capital markets remain deepest and most liquid

If a crisis occurs in the 2026-2030 window (increasingly likely given US fiscal position), dollar would likely strengthen — opposite of dedollarization thesis.

**Variable #6: Political Will in Key Countries**

The report treats dedollarization as an economic inevitability. But:

- China's Communist Party prioritizes domestic stability over international monetary ambition
- Saudi Arabia values the US security guarantee above any financial diversification
- India values dollar stability given its large external debt in dollars
- Russia is motivated by sanctions but is a small economy

**Variable #7: Dollar Pricing Power in Commodities**

The analysis barely addresses commodity pricing. Oil remains dollar-denominated not because of SWIFT or banking infrastructure but because:

- OPEC prices in dollars (Saudi decision)
- Commodity traders invoice in dollars
- No alternative exchange with sufficient liquidity exists

Changing commodity pricing requires changing where commodity traders, insurers, and shipping companies operate — not just building payment infrastructure.

### The Core Weakness

The analysis conflates **narrative momentum** with **structural change**. The dedollarization narrative is stronger in 2026 than in 2016 — but that doesn't mean the underlying economics have changed proportionally. Every data point supporting the thesis (mBridge growth, gold buying, Treasury selling) has a plausible alternative explanation that the analysis dismisses too quickly.

The 2026-2030 window is described as "critical" but the specific mechanisms that would make it decisive are:

1. US fiscal crisis (not base case)
2. Credible replacement currency (not possible given BRICS conflicts)
3. Saudi oil pricing change (unconfirmed and contradicted by behavior)

Without one of these catalysts, the base case is continued gradualism — and gradualism has been the story for 50 years without triggering phase transition.
