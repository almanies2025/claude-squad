## Discovery: BRICS Cannot Agree on What to Build — Only What to Tear Down

**What was found**: BRICS has 11 members with fundamentally incompatible monetary preferences. BRICS Pay was announced in 2024 with fanfare — **it is not operational as a unified system**. There is no common settlement layer, no common currency, no agreed reserve asset. China wants yuan dominance. India wants balance. Brazil wants real. Saudi Arabia wants dollar hedging. Russia wants anything anti-dollar.

**Why it matters**: The dedollarization narrative often treats BRICS as a coherent alternative bloc. It is not. It is a political coalition united by opposition to dollar hegemony — and divided by every dimension of what would replace it.

**The institutional gap**: The EU took 50+ years to build the euro (from EEC to Maastricht to euro launch). BRICS has existed for 15 years and has built no common financial infrastructure. The idea that BRICS Pay becomes a SWIFT replacement in 5-10 years is not credible.

**What BRICS countries are actually building**: _parallel_ systems — not a unified alternative:

- China-Russia: fully dedollarized via CIPS bilateral corridors
- China-Gulf: mBridge (UAE + Saudi participants)
- Russia-India: growing bilateral via local currency arrangements

**The structural implication**: Dedollarization is happening _within_ BRICS, between aligned pairs, through bilateral infrastructure. It is not happening through a unified BRICS system. This means the dollar is being bypassed in corridors, not replaced globally.

**Sources**: GIS Reports (Oct 2025), Lowy Institute, Chicago Policy Review, Taylor & Francis (L.A. Johnston)

## For Discussion

1. BRICS expansion to 11 members — did adding Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Iran together make the coalition more or less capable of building unified infrastructure? (More economic weight, but more incompatible interests.)
2. Is the absence of a unified BRICS alternative actually _better_ for dedollarization than a unified system would be? (Bilateral bypasses may be more resilient than a single point of failure.)
3. China's strategy appears to be: build yuan-denominated infrastructure (CIPS, mBridge) and let other countries choose to use it rather than forcing consensus. Is this more effective than BRICS-wide unification?
