## GAP: Capital Account Convertibility Is the Binding Constraint on RMB Internationalization

**What was found**: The analysis at 02-payment-infrastructure.md discusses CIPS and mBridge as infrastructure enabling RMB internationalization. It never mentions China's capital controls as a binding constraint on RMB reserve currency status.

**The fundamental problem**: A currency cannot be a global reserve currency without capital account convertibility — meaning residents and non-residents can freely convert it, move it across borders, and hold it without restriction. China's capital account is NOT fully convertible:

- Individual annual RMB conversion limits exist
- Institutional capital flows are regulated via quotas (QFII, RQFII, Stock Connect, Bond Connect)
- Cross-border capital movements require approval for many transaction types
- PBOC maintains extensive FX intervention capability

**Why this is a hard ceiling**: You cannot have global RMB reserve currency status while maintaining capital controls. The Communist Party will not liberalize capital account convertibility because:

1. It would enable capital flight by domestic Chinese (wealthy individuals, corporations)
2. It would reduce PBOC's ability to manage yuan exchange rate
3. It would create domestic financial instability risk
4. It would undermine the Party's control over the domestic financial system

**The analysis's error**: The analysis at 03-petrodollar-mechanism.md states "China's record surplus means more RMB available for trade settlement" — this confuses trade surplus with reserve currency status. Trade surplus countries (Germany, Japan historically) accumulate reserves; they don't distribute reserve currencies.

**Reserve currency issuers** (US, historically UK) run current account deficits and send currency abroad. China runs current account surpluses — meaning RMB flows INTO China, not OUT from it. This is structurally inconsistent with RMB as a global reserve currency.

**What the analysis should have addressed**: The fundamental contradiction — China's political economy cannot accept the conditions necessary for RMB to become a global reserve currency.

## For Discussion

1. If China's capital controls prevent RMB internationalization, what is the realistic ceiling for RMB's global reserve share? (China's own trade settlement in RMB, plus bilateral corridors with aligned governments — not global reserve status.)
2. Does CIPS/mBridge growth represent genuine RMB internationalization or just RMB-denominated transactions within China's controlled financial system?
3. Is it possible that dedollarization proceeds through small currencies and gold rather than through a successor reserve currency — and is this actually consistent with the "multipolar" thesis rather than a yuan-dominant system?
