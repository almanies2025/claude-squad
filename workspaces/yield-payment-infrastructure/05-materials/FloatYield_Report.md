## Executive Summary

FloatYield is a B2B infrastructure platform enabling banks and fintechs to offer interest-bearing payment accounts. The platform manages Treasury reserve assets, calculates and distributes yield, and handles regulatory reporting through the Sponsor Bank model.

### The Core Opportunity

US banks earn **$50–80B annually** in float income on transaction balances. Customers receive 0%. Stablecoins (USDC, USDT, $150B+ combined AUM) prove customers will shift behavior for yield — and earn their issuers ~$6–7B/year in seigniorage. Yet no B2B infrastructure platform exists to let banks and fintechs offer competitive yield on payment accounts at scale.

| Metric                        | Value                                              |
| ----------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| TAM (total US float income)   | $50–80B/year                                       |
| SAM (B2B platform accessible) | $500M–2B/year                                      |
| SOM (Year 3, base case)       | $4–10M/year                                        |
| Direct competitor             | Stablecoins ($150B AUM)                            |
| B2B infrastructure competitor | None (Unit/Column partial)                         |
| Market timing                 | Optimal — 4.5% Treasury yield makes economics work |

### The Critical Finding: Two Structural Flaws

**Flaw #1: Reconciliation Fee Misalignment**
The original fee model ($0.10–0.25/account/month) creates a structural mismatch: revenue scales with **accounts**, while reconciliation liability scales with **deposits**. At Year 3 scale (1M accounts, $1B deposits), 30–75% of revenue is consumed by reconciliation losses. The annual reconciliation gap on a $1B book at 4.5% is approximately **$900K/year**.

**Flaw #2: Sponsor Bank Structural Conflict**
At scale ($500M+ deposits), the Sponsor Bank bears FDIC exposure and compliance burden while FloatYield captures most economic upside. The bank has structural incentive to build a competing product or acquire a competitor. Sponsor Bank termination is an existential threat.

### The Fixes

| Flaw                             | Fix                                                         | Result                                             |
| -------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Reconciliation Fee Misalignment  | Switch to basis points (bps) on Average Daily Balance (ADB) | Revenue scales with float; 5.6× coverage at Year 3 |
| Sponsor Bank Structural Conflict | CUSO (Credit Union Service Organization) structure          | Removes bank dependency; aligned incentives        |

**PROCEED.** The market gap is real, the regulatory path is clear (Sponsor Bank model, 3–6 months to MVP), and the competitive landscape has no pure-play B2B infrastructure winner.

**The single most important action before building: secure the Sponsor Bank partnership.** Everything else depends on it.

## Section 2: Product & Architecture

### 2.1 Sponsor Bank Architecture

```
Customer deposits → Sponsor Bank (holds at Fed) → Bank invests in Treasuries
Yield accrues → FloatYield calculates daily yield → Bank distributes to customer
FloatYield takes platform fee → monthly
```

**Critical rule:** FloatYield must NEVER hold customer funds directly.

### 2.2 Yield Calculation

**Core formula:**

```
daily_yield = balance × annual_rate × days_elapsed / 365
```

**Rate tiers:**

| Tier      | Balance Range        | Annual Rate |
| --------- | -------------------- | ----------- |
| Standard  | $0 – $9,999.99       | 4.25%       |
| Preferred | $10,000 – $49,999.99 | 4.50%       |
| Premier   | $50,000+             | 4.75%       |

### 2.3 The Reconciliation Problem

FloatYield uses 365-day year; Treasuries use actual/actual. On $1B at 4.5%:

| Scenario | Daily | 30-Day | 90-Day |
| -------- | ----- | ------ | ------ |
| 1bp      | $100K | $300K  | $900K  |
| 2bp      | $200K | $600K  | $1.8M  |
| 3bp      | $300K | $900K  | $2.7M  |

**Annual expected divergence:** ~$900K/year on $1B (0.09% of notional) — not a rounding error.

### 2.4 Five Architecture Decisions

| ADR | Decision                  | Rationale                              |
| --- | ------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| 001 | Sponsor Bank Model        | FloatYield = tech provider, not bank   |
| 002 | Reserve Custody           | Bank holds at Fed; FloatYield monitors |
| 003 | Daily calc / Monthly dist | Precision + simplified reconciliation  |
| 004 | Bank owns KYC/AML         | Regulatory obligation belongs to bank  |
| 005 | REST API + webhooks       | Bank-friendly; MVP-appropriate         |

## Section 4: Business Model Redesign

### 4.1 The Two Flaws

**Flaw #1:** Revenue ($0.10–0.25/account/mo) scales with accounts; liability ($900K/yr per $1B) scales with deposits.

**Flaw #2:** Bank bears FDIC risk; FloatYield captures upside. At scale, bank has incentive to build alternatives.

### 4.2 Fix #1: Tiered bps on ADB

| Tier   | ADB      | Fee    |
| ------ | -------- | ------ |
| Pilot  | <$50M    | 75 bps |
| Growth | $50–500M | 65 bps |
| Scale  | >$500M   | 50 bps |

**Year 3:** $7.5M revenue / $1.35M liability = **5.6× coverage**

### 4.3 Fix #2: CUSO Structure

- Partner with credit unions instead of banks
- NCUA-insured (not FDIC — equivalent)
- Credit unions need loanable deposits — aligned incentives
- ~$1.3T in credit union deposits nationally

### 4.4 Alternative: ILC Charter (Long-term)

| Aspect   | Detail                              |
| -------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Cost     | $10M+ capital                       |
| Timeline | 18–36 months                        |
| Benefit  | Eliminates bank dependency entirely |

## Section 5: Red Team Analysis

### 5.1 Sponsor Bank Existential Risk

At $500M+ deposits:

1. Bank realizes FloatYield captures most economic value
2. Regulators pay attention to material BaaS programs
3. Bank builds competing product or acquires competitor

**Termination = FloatYield's core product disappears.**

### 5.2 CUSO Timeline Risk

- 6–12 month timeline is optimistic; expect **12–18 months**
- Pool of eligible credit unions: ~200–400 institutions
- $1.5B Year 3 ADB target is aggressive; realistic: $600M

### 5.3 ILC Capital Risk

- Total capital needed: **$4–8M** (application + operating runway)
- ILC denial rate (2024–2026): ~50%
- Model has no contingency for denial

### 5.4 Fatal Flaw: Cash Flow Timing Collapse

1. ILC charter needs $4–8M capital
2. Capital raised from projected CUSO fee revenue
3. CUSO partnerships take 12–18 months (not 6–12)
4. Fee revenue delayed → runway depleted
5. ILC application lapses → model collapses

### 5.5 Risk Register

| Risk                                        | Likelihood  | Impact   |
| ------------------------------------------- | ----------- | -------- |
| Reconciliation divergence ($900K/yr on $1B) | High        | Critical |
| CUSO partnership >18 months                 | High        | Critical |
| ILC charter denied                          | Medium      | Critical |
| Sponsor Bank termination at Year 2–3        | Medium-High | Critical |
| Interest rate decline (<3.5% Treasury)      | Medium      | Critical |
| Large bank replicates                       | Medium      | High     |
